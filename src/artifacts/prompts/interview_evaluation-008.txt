The output was a ranked list that sales could see and critique. We disagreed on prioritization about 40% of the time. But the disagreements were about data, not instinct, which made them resolvable.

[00:22] Elliot (Interviewer): What's your theory of how product organizations should be structured? And where do you think most companies get it wrong?

[00:23] Maria (Candidate): Most companies structure product orgs by function — one PM per feature area or engineering team. That works fine tactically. Where it breaks down is strategic coherence. You end up with PMs who are excellent feature owners but have no shared view of the customer journey, and no one is accountable for the end-to-end experience. At Prism, I restructured from functional alignment to customer journey alignment. PMs owned segments of the customer lifecycle — acquisition, onboarding, expansion — rather than engineering teams. The adjustment was painful: it required remapping PM scope, renegotiating relationships with engineering leads, and one PM who couldn't adapt eventually left. But within a year, we saw a meaningful change in NPS for the onboarding segment specifically — it went from 42 to 61 — because one person was accountable for the entire journey, not a handoff between three PMs.

I've actively worked on this — I've pushed myself to articulate longer-term bets and defend them — but I don't think I've fully closed the gap.

[00:35] Elliot (Interviewer): That's candid. Let me follow up: if you had to name a three-to-five year product bet for a company in this space, what would it be?

Then I'd build the thesis from that base. I'm not comfortable speculating without that foundation.

[00:40] Marcus (Interviewer): Fair enough. What questions do you have for us?

[00:41] Maria (Candidate): Two for Marcus — how mature is the current engineering organization's capacity to estimate work reliably, and how does your team currently feel about the product organization's ability to make prioritization decisions they can trust? [00:45] Elliot (Interviewer): One more from me. You mentioned you've been told you're better at short-term than long-term vision. If we hired you and that was still true at 18 months, what would that look like and what would you do about it?

[00:46] Maria (Candidate): I've thought about this. What I'd do: first, name it explicitly to you rather than hoping it doesn't get noticed. Second, actively create forcing functions — a quarterly "where is the category in three years" workshop with leadership, external advisor input, committed roadmap space for speculative bets. [00:51] Nina (Interviewer): How do you think about pricing and packaging as a product responsibility?

[00:52] Maria (Candidate): As one I'm under-invested in by most product leaders. Pricing is the translation layer between value delivered and revenue captured. At Prism, packaging was a mess when I joined — one plan with everything, so small customers overpaid and enterprise customers felt no differentiation. I led a packaging redesign over nine months: three tiers, with the mid tier engineered to convert SMB customers who had outgrown Starter but didn't need full Enterprise. I pulled feature usage by account size and found a natural break at 15 seats with three specific features. The redesign launched in Q4 2022. Within two quarters, mid-tier conversion from SMB increased 22% and average contract value for that segment went from $8,400 to $12,600 annually. This was a joint project with the CFO and sales ops — I owned the product tier definition, they owned the pricing model.
